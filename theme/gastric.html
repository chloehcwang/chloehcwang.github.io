All kids had experience of occasional stomach ache. However, the diagnosis has never been easy because children find it challenging to adequately articulate their symptoms. Even some parents feel uncertain on how to address their child’s pain as well.

The brief

This is my first project working at NIHI (the National Institute for Health Innovation ).

The task is to design and build a paediatrics version of Alimetryᵀᴹ app. To enable children, aged 5 -17 years, to record gastric symptoms once a day, for 7 days before clinic day .

The adult Alimetryᵀᴹ app is only for recording symptoms on the clinic day, the task needs to add a new user flow of symptom tracking for 7 days, as it gives a more accurate picture over time.

Key Goal

Converting the existing adult version app to a children's version, with a new 7 days tracking user flow.

The aim is to capture and record data for research analyses and in the end helping to diagnose and inform personalized therapy.

The APP needs to be clear and simple for the little ones to use. The UI design should be based on the adult version with the same logo and branding colour.

Gastric Alimetryᵀᴹ

The challenge

Coping techniques to help kids thrive

  • Adult app currently not suitable and engaging for paediatric use
  • Children struggle to understand how to rate pain severity using a 0-10 scale
  • Children find it diificult to understand the difference between all 10 symptoms
  • Design an app that is engaging for 5-8 age group, but not boring for 9-14 age group

The impact

A positive start...and still Day One

After completing the initial version, I moved on to a new project. However, I later found out from my colleagues that the first version of our product was being used by specialists and PhDs, with over two dozen paediatric patients benefiting from it.

While our clients were pleased with the valuable data collected, issues were also identified, including the need to redesign the admin dashboard and accommodate the varying needs of older and younger children.

EMPATHIZE

Internal research

By reading the given documents and adult app prototype, we get to know that Gastric Alimetryᵀᴹ is a non-invasive device that is worn in the clinic.

The device delivers new validated biomarkers of gastric function, based on multi-award-winning scientific innovation.

When combined with the recording of symptoms and feelings via the app, the Gastric Alimetryᵀᴹ technology aids in diagnosing and therapy.

Stakeholder interview

In order to identify key goals and gather context and history. We facilitated a stakeholder interview.

After the interview, we had a clearer vision of the goal and a better understanding of the current app.

Then we listed the pain points together.

Developed a three-week plan using a Trello board.

Secondary Research

Consider physical & cognitive ability

As an ECE teacher before, I noticed children in pre-school age can do some simple clicks but are more into dragging or sliding. Children's fine motor skills and cognitive development vary in thier early stages.

So I led a secondary research regarding children’s physical & cognitive capability towards using mobiles or tablets.

Overcoming user research and testing constraints

The real users are children with gastric symptoms. But we've been told we were unable to run any tests with these kids. So the first challenge I encountered was how to empathise with users and validate hypothesis without access to real users ?

To overcome the challenge, we first conducted external secondary research. We looked for academic journals, websites, articles, and videos in order to collect information about UX practice for children.

We also decide to look for normal children to conduct usability tests.

An Interesting finding for the next version

At first, I only knew that designing for children should consider fine motor skills and cognitive capability from my own experience.

But with more research, there is a consistent finding of the need to target very narrow age groups when designing for children. There's no such thing as "designing rules for children," defined as everybody for all age ranges.

Each group has different behaviours and physical and cognitive capabilities. Young users reacted negatively to content designed for children who were even one school grade below or above their own level

So I suggested to design the app into two version with age 5 - 8 and age 9 -17. The client, however, prefers to start with just one version for all ages. After further discussion, we came to agree to stick with the current plan and make the product in 2 age groups in the future version 2.

With this in mind, I tried to make my designs compatible with different age groups of children. I also tested this issue in the usability test to gather evidence and data for version 2 iteration.

Competitor Analysis

The aim is to make the app easier and more engaging for children to use. We found that there are no direct competitors in the market, which is good.

We then expanded the scope of the research to include health rating apps and children using health apps.

We found that kids' health apps tend to use games and rewards to keep kids coming back.

DEFINE

Combined all the pain points we listed together, we surmised as a problem statement.

Problem Statement

The existing gastric app is used by adults to track and rate the severity of their gastric symptoms; however, it is not suitable for use by children (5 – 18 years old). The current app is only for use at the clinic, it has no ability to track symptoms in the week leading up to clinic day

Because children have a different level of health literacy, they may not understand the ratings, making them inaccurate. Without keeping track of seven days, the participants must recall the symptoms they experienced during the week, which is more difficult for children to do.

As a result, it is unlikely the researcher can get accurate information during the 7 days leading up to clinic day, so the diagnosis may be inaccurate.

Keep it intuitive

  • Visualizing severity rating

The symptoms page is the most challenging since young children are unable to rate pain severity using numbers.
All designers shared similar thoughts on using emoji faces to represent the scales 0 -10. But the main difference of my design is that I placed the rating bar vertically rather than horizontally.

  • Matching the real world

As my experience in ECE, toddlers can understand the concept of high and low. So I suggested to place the rating bar vertically. It is easier for children to understand that dragging up means a high rating because in the real world they know up means high.

  • More accurate results for older kids

I also kept the numbers besides, for those older kids who can rate severity more precisely by using numbers.

  • Adding voice guidance by audio play

Critical for the youngest age groups to be able to use the app.

Making each recording a reward

My idea was once the kids completed their recording, there will be a monster show up on the screen.
So in this way makes this repeated recording task more fun and keeps kids returning to unlock the next monster..

Reflection


  • Overcoming user research and testing constraints

    • One challenge I encountered in this project was how to empathize and validate hypotheses without access to real users.

    Despite being a little disappointed at first, I quickly realized that this was a learning opportunity, because I believe it is not unusual that a project may have limitations like tight budgets or deadlines.

    To overcome this problem, I first conducted internal research by asking for documents of gastric symptoms, adult version prototype, and its user test results. I then conducted secondary research by browsing the web looking for UX design rules for kids.

    All the research allowed me to stand on the shoulders of giants.

  • Collaboration is the key to great results

    • This project was entirely collaborative, meaning every part of our final product came from input from each other, our diligent developers, and the professional feedback of PhDs and professors.
